To What Extent Do Citrus Fruits Contribute to Enamel Demineralization?

PublishedMarch 14, 2026
AuthorYour Name
CategoryBiology
Read the full PDF ↗ Download PDF

Abstract

This study investigates the erosive effect of common citrus fruits on tooth enamel by measuring surface hardness change after controlled acid exposure, comparing lemon, orange, and grapefruit juice against a water control.

Research question

To what extent does exposure to citrus fruit juice contribute to enamel demineralization compared to a neutral control, and does this effect vary by fruit type?

Methodology

Extracted bovine enamel samples were divided into four groups and immersed in lemon, orange, grapefruit juice, or distilled water for a fixed exposure period. Surface microhardness was measured before and after exposure using a Vickers hardness tester, and percentage change in hardness was compared across groups using one-way ANOVA.

Key findings

All three citrus juices produced statistically significant enamel softening relative to the water control (p < 0.05), with lemon juice producing the largest mean hardness loss, consistent with its lower pH. Orange juice produced the smallest effect among the three fruits tested.

Significance

The results support existing dental health guidance to limit direct, prolonged citrus exposure to enamel and rinse with water afterward, and offer a simple comparative reference for relative erosive risk across common citrus fruits.
